Waveform Studio

Edit audio down to the single sample

A waveform is the visual representation of your audio — peaks show loud moments, troughs show silence. Zenframe lets you zoom in to individual samples (1/44,100th of a second) for surgical precision.

What is a waveform?
A waveform is a graph of sound over time. The height of each point represents volume — tall spikes are loud transients like drums or speech consonants, flat areas are silence or sustained notes.
What is sample-level editing?
Audio is made of thousands of individual samples per second (usually 44,100 or 48,000). Editing at the sample level means you can remove a click, trim a breath, or fix a harsh sibilant to sub-millisecond accuracy.
What are markers & regions?
Markers are flags you drop at key moments (e.g. "start of chorus"). Regions are spans of audio you group together — useful for looping a section, muting a chunk, or applying bulk edits.

Mixing Console

A full studio mixer,
inside your browser

The mixing console is where individual tracks come together into a cohesive sound. Think of it like a physical mixing board — each channel strip controls one track, and the master controls the final output going to your ears and your export.

What is a channel strip?
A channel strip is a vertical column of controls for a single track. From top to bottom: input gain, EQ, compression, effects sends, pan knob, and fader. Every track gets its own strip — that's how you shape each element independently.
What is a fader?
The fader is the main volume slider for a track. Automation lets you record fader movements over time — e.g. fade in a guitar at bar 8, dip the music under dialogue, then bring it back up.
What is the master bus?
The master bus is the final stereo output — every track feeds into it. Apply limiting and metering here to ensure your mix doesn't clip and hits the right loudness level for your platform.

AI Retouching Suite

Retouch like a pro,
in seconds

Professional retouching used to take hours. Zenframe's AI understands skin, eyes, hair, and objects — so it knows what to smooth, sharpen, or remove without destroying the natural look of your subject.

What is frequency separation?
Your photo has two layers of information: texture (pores, hair, fabric grain) and color (skin tone, shadows, highlights). Frequency separation splits these so you can fix blotchy skin tone without erasing natural texture — the result looks real, not airbrushed.
What is dodge & burn?
Dodging lightens areas to add dimension; burning darkens to add depth. Pro retouchers use it to sculpt cheekbones, define jawlines, and remove distracting shadows. Zenframe lets you paint these adjustments with a pressure-sensitive brush.

Color Grading

Turn flat photos into
cinematic art

Color grading is the difference between a snapshot and a photograph. It's how you set the mood, fix exposure problems, and make every image feel like it belongs to a consistent visual story.

What are curves?
A curve is a graph with input brightness on the X axis and output brightness on the Y axis. Pull the middle up to brighten midtones. Drag the top-right down to recover blown highlights. Pull the bottom-left up to add a lifted shadow look (the "faded film" effect). RGB curves let you adjust each colour channel independently for precise colour casts.
What is a LUT?
A LUT (Look Up Table) is a colour formula — it maps every input colour to a specific output colour. Professional colourists sell LUTs as "film emulation packs" or "cinematic looks". Drop a LUT onto your photo and it instantly shifts the colour palette to match a specific mood or film stock — then you fine-tune from there.
What is tone mapping?
Real scenes have a huge range of brightness — from deep shadow to blazing sun. Camera sensors can't capture it all. Tone mapping is the process of compressing that range into what a screen can display, while preserving detail in both shadows and highlights. Essential for landscape and architectural photography.
🔄
Advanced Curves
Master RGB curves, individual channel curves, and hue-vs-saturation curves. The point curve panel shows your image's histogram underneath so you can see exactly which tones you're adjusting.
🎞️
LUT Library

The Timeline

What is a timeline and
how does it work?

The timeline is the heart of video editing — it's a horizontal map of your entire video laid out in time, left to right. Every clip, audio track, title, and effect sits on the timeline as a block you can move, trim, and layer.

What are tracks?
Tracks are horizontal lanes stacked on top of each other. Video tracks sit above audio tracks. The top video track is what you see on screen — if a clip on Track 2 overlaps a clip on Track 1, Track 2 appears in front. This is how you layer text, graphics, and picture-in-picture.
What is a ripple edit?
When you trim the end of a clip, a ripple edit automatically shifts every subsequent clip left to fill the gap — so you never get blank black frames in the middle of your video. It keeps everything in sync as you make cuts.
What is a keyframe?
A keyframe is a point in time where you set a specific value — like opacity 0% at second 2, and opacity 100% at second 3. Zenframe automatically interpolates between keyframes to create smooth animations for position, scale, rotation, and any effect parameter.
What is magnetic snapping?
When you drag a clip near another clip's edge, it "snaps" into place like a magnet — ensuring zero gaps and perfectly aligned cuts without manually zooming in to check. You can toggle snapping on/off with the S key.
📋
Multi-Track Timeline
Unlimited video and audio tracks with magnetic snapping, ripple edits, and slip/slide trimming. Colour-code tracks by camera, speaker, or scene for complex projects.
🗂️

Effects & Transitions

Make every cut
feel intentional

Transitions and effects aren't just decorations — they control how your audience feels as they move through your story. A hard cut creates energy; a slow dissolve creates calm. Zenframe gives you 200+ tools to match every mood.

What is a transition?
A transition is an animation between two clips. The most common is a straight cut (no animation). Others include: dissolve (clip A fades out while clip B fades in), wipe (B slides over A), zoom (smash cut with scale), and dip-to-black (fade to black then up again). Each tells a slightly different story.
What is motion blur?
When a fast object moves, your eye sees a blur trail — think a spinning logo or fast camera pan. Zenframe can add synthetic motion blur to any clip or animation to make movement look natural and cinematic, even if the original footage was shot without it.
What are lower thirds?
Lower thirds are text graphics that appear in the bottom portion of the screen — used to display a person's name and title in interviews, location captions, or subtitles. Zenframe has 40+ animated lower third templates you can customise with your own brand fonts and colours.

Export & Delivery

Get your video
out to the world

The export stage is where your project becomes a shareable file. The right settings determine file size, image quality, and whether your video looks great on every screen — from a phone to a cinema projector.

What is a codec? (H.264, ProRes…)
A codec is an algorithm that compresses your video into a smaller file. H.264 is the internet standard — small files, great compatibility. H.265 (HEVC) halves the size again. ProRes is used in professional post-production — much larger files but lossless quality for editing handoffs. Choose H.264 for YouTube; ProRes for delivering to a client.
What does bitrate mean?
Bitrate is how much data is used per second of video, measured in Mbps. Higher bitrate = sharper image but larger file. YouTube recommends 8 Mbps for 1080p, 35–45 Mbps for 4K. Zenframe's platform presets set this automatically — or you can dial it in manually.
What is colour space / profile?
Colour profiles (sRGB, Rec.709, DCI-P3) define the range of colours encoded in the file. Rec.709 is standard for web and broadcast. DCI-P3 is cinema. If you shoot in LOG or RAW, Zenframe converts to the correct output colour space automatically so colours look right everywhere.
